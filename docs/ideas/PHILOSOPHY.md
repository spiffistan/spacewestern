# Design Philosophy

Not systems or mechanics — ideas about what kind of experience the game should create. The feel, the tone, the moments that matter.

## Permanence

Most colony sims let you undo mistakes cheaply. What if some decisions were irreversible?

- You built here. You can't move the colony.
- You exiled that colonist. They might come back wearing enemy colors.
- You dug too deep. The water will never stop.
- You burned the forest. It won't grow back for years.
- You didn't stockpile food. Winter is here. It's too late.

The western frontier was full of irreversible gambles. The game should feel like that. Not punishing — consequential. Every decision writes history.

## The Map as Memory

Every playthrough's map tells a story through physical marks:

- Worn paths between the mine and smelter (terrain compaction)
- Scorched earth where the great fire happened (burn scars)
- The graveyard on the hill (placed tombstones)
- The collapsed tunnel nobody talks about (rubble-filled passage)
- Tree stumps where the forest used to be
- Foundations of buildings that were destroyed in the second raid

A year-old colony's map looks nothing like a fresh one. Every mark has a story. The map IS the narrative — you don't need a quest log because you can SEE what happened.

## Loneliness and Scale

Small colonies should feel qualitatively different from large ones.

**2-3 colonists:** Intimate. Every person matters enormously. One death is catastrophic — not just for labor, but for the survivor's mental state. Two people alone on a frontier planet depend on each other completely. Meals together. Shared watch duty. The silence when one is gone.

**5-7 colonists:** A community forming. Specialization begins. Social dynamics emerge — cliques, rivalries, friendships. The colony has a personality.

**10+ colonists:** A settlement. Anonymous relationships become possible. The player can't track everyone individually. Management abstractions matter. The shift from "I know each person" to "I manage a population."

A single colonist alone should be a genuinely different experience. Quiet. Focused. Fragile. Desperate for any new arrival. The loneliest possible western.

## The Things You Can't Build

Not everything is craftable. Some things only come from the land:

- A natural hot spring for bathing (mood buff, healing)
- A scenic overlook that boosts mood when visited
- An ancient tree that provides shade and beauty
- A natural cave that's already sheltered
- A river crossing that enables trade
- Fertile soil that grows better crops

The best colony sites aren't just resource-rich — they have natural features worth building around. You adapt to the land, not just reshape it. Choosing WHERE to settle is the first and most permanent decision.

## Stories Around the Fire

Colonists gathered near a fireplace at night share stories — generated from actual game events:

- "Remember when the basement flooded and we lost all the food?"
- "Jeb was the bravest in that raid. He held the door alone."
- "I still think about the one who left. She had the right idea."

Stories reinforce bonds between those present. They process trauma (reduce negative memory duration). They create a shared history that makes the colony feel like a community, not a labor pool.

The campfire is the colony's heart. Its warmth isn't just thermal — it's emotional.

## Scarcity as Teacher

Each crisis teaches a lesson:

- First winter → you learn food storage matters
- First drought → you learn water infrastructure matters
- First raid → you learn walls matter
- First cave-in → you learn supports matter
- First disease → you learn ventilation matters

The game shouldn't TELL you these things. The systems should teach through consequence. A colony that survived its first winter is fundamentally different from one that hasn't — not just in stockpiles but in the player's understanding.

No tutorials. No tip popups. The world is the teacher. Failure is the curriculum.

## The Weight of Command

The player is making decisions that affect fictional lives. Occasionally, the game should make you feel that weight:

- Send someone into a flooded mine? They might not come back.
- Ration food in winter? Someone goes hungry.
- Exile a troublemaker? They have nowhere to go.

Not as a mechanic — as a moment. A colonist looks at you (breaks the fourth wall? or speaks to the leader?) and asks: "Are you sure?" Then you click yes, and whatever happens is on you.

These moments should be rare. One per crisis, maybe. But they should land.

## Echoes

People who leave don't just vanish.

- An exiled colonist joins the Redskulls. Raids your colony wearing their old hat.
- A colonist who died has their name on a tombstone. New arrivals ask about them.
- An abandoned outpost crumbles into ruins — you see it on the map, a reminder of overreach.
- A trade caravan mentions your colony by name. Your reputation echoes back.

Actions have afterlives. The world remembers what you did, even when you've moved on.

## Silence

Not every moment needs content. A quiet afternoon — colonists working steadily, sun tracking across the sky, wind in the grass — IS the game.

The tension comes from knowing it won't last. The storm is coming. The Redskulls are out there. Winter approaches. But right now, in this moment, everything is okay.

The most powerful moments in a colony sim are the calm before the storm. Don't fill every second with events, notifications, crises. Let the player breathe. Let them watch the sunset over the colony they built. Let them listen to the wind.

Then take it away.

## The Colony Is the Character

In an RPG, the character grows and changes. Here, the colony IS the character.

- It has a personality (the sum of its people)
- It has a history (the map's scars and stories)
- It has strengths and weaknesses (what you invested in vs. neglected)
- It has a reputation (how the world sees it)
- It can die (total wipe) or endure (through generational change)

The player doesn't play AS a character. They play as the invisible force that guides a community. The colony's story is the player's story — told through buildings, paths, graves, and the people who survived.
