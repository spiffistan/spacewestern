# The Setting

What lies beyond the colony. Who these people are. Why they're here. What happened to everything else. This is the lore foundation that every other doc hangs on — the answer to "why does any of this matter?"

## The Tone in One Sentence

Civilization existed, and it receded. You are far from everything you knew, and it isn't coming back.

---

## The Reach

Humanity expanded across a cluster of star systems over the course of two centuries. Not a unified empire — more like the age of European colonization. Competing interests: corporate charters, military expeditions, religious settler groups, idealist communes, and desperate refugees all sent ships outward. Travel between stars is slow — weeks or months in transit, communication at ship speed. News from the inner systems reaches the frontier months late. A war in one system isn't known in another until the next ship arrives.

The inner systems — Earth, its first colonies, the industrial hubs — are densely populated, technologically advanced, politically complex. The outer systems are thin: scattered settlements on marginally habitable worlds, mining stations, research outposts, failed colonies. The further out you go, the less anything works. The supply chains stretch. The replacement parts don't come. The law is whatever the strongest settlement says it is.

The Reach is not Star Trek. It's not even Star Wars. It's the British Empire at the limits of its telegraph lines: a veneer of order in the core, and chaos at the edges.

## The Charters

Colonization is funded by charter. Someone — a company, a government, a church, a wealthy idealist — buys the right to settle a surveyed world. They fund a ship, fill it with colonists, equipment, seed stock, and supplies for the first two years. The colonists sign a contract: in exchange for passage and provisions, they work the land, build the settlement, and send back whatever the charter holder wants — ore, data, agricultural products, proof that humans can live there.

Different charters produce different cultures:

**Corporate charters** are the most common. A mining company sends engineers and laborers to extract ore. Efficient, well-equipped, soulless. The colonists are employees, not citizens. When the ore dries up, the company pulls out. The people left behind aren't the company's problem.

**Military charters** establish outposts on strategic worlds. Disciplined, structured, with clear chains of command. Good at defense, bad at adaptation. When the military moves on, the remaining personnel either maintain discipline (becoming a rigid, authoritarian settlement) or fragment.

**Religious charters** are communities of faith seeking a world of their own. Tight social bonds, shared purpose, internal cohesion. But insular, suspicious of outsiders, sometimes rigid in ways that don't serve survival. The Preacher backstory comes from one of these.

**Idealist charters** are the dreamers — communes, utopian experiments, research collectives. Smart, creative, terrible at defense. They produce the best thinkers and the most vulnerable settlements.

**Desperate charters** are barely charters at all — refugee ships, prison transports, the dispossessed buying passage on whatever floats. No plan, no equipment, no unity. Just people who had no other option. The Drifter and the Convict come from here.

Your colonists are a cross-section. The Manifest (chargen.md) puts a Mechanic from a corporate tech mission next to a Preacher from a religious commune next to an Outlaw from... wherever. They didn't choose each other. They're what's left.

## This Planet

Catalogued as **LV-726** in the survey records. Habitable: breathable atmosphere, liquid water, temperate zones, no detected pathogens. Flagged as "suitable for agricultural settlement" by an automated probe sixty years ago. Nobody looked more closely. Nobody asked what made the terrain so regular, or why the atmospheric composition was so perfectly calibrated, or what the subsurface thermal signatures meant.

A corporate charter — the **Weyland-Sutter Colonial Group** — purchased settlement rights and outfitted the *Perdition*, a mid-range colony ship carrying 600 souls, prefab structures, agricultural equipment, seed banks, mining rigs, and a two-year supply buffer. The Perdition arrived 47 years ago.

### The First Colony

First Landing — later called **Perdition Point** — was built on a river plain in the temperate belt. For eight years, it worked. The colony grew crops, extracted minerals, sent cargo shipments back on the annual supply run. Population grew to nearly a thousand with the second wave.

Then something shifted. The creatures — previously rare, mostly nocturnal, avoidable — became more aggressive. Thermogasts drawn to the settlement's growing heat signature. Duskweaver packs growing bolder as their habitat was pushed back. The hollowcall, which the first settlers had dismissed as "wind through canyons," began waking people at night. Colonists reported dreams. Stress mounted.

Year 9: a catastrophic thermogast incursion breached the perimeter during a cold snap. 43 dead. Major structural damage. The colony held, but trust in the leadership fractured.

Year 11: a faction split. Half the colony — the ones who wanted to fortify and dig in — stayed at Perdition Point. The other half — the ones who wanted to spread out and reduce their thermal footprint — left to establish smaller settlements. Contact was maintained by radio.

Year 14: the annual supply ship didn't come. The colony waited. Year 15: still nothing. Year 16: a garbled transmission from the inner systems — something about a "quarantine" or "closure." The word "war" might have been in there. Then static. Then nothing.

### The Decline

Without resupply, everything contracted. Medicine ran out. Replacement parts ran out. The fabrication equipment, designed to be supported by shipments of rare materials, produced lower-quality goods with local substitutes. Skills were lost as specialists died without training replacements. The original colonists' children grew up knowing less than their parents about the technology that kept them alive.

Perdition Point, the largest settlement, held on the longest. It's unclear when it finally fell — sometime around year 30. Radio contact with the outlying settlements became sporadic, then ceased. Each group was left with whatever they had: their people, their knowledge, their immediate resources.

Now — year 47 since landing, roughly 30 years since the Silence — the planet has a few hundred humans scattered across a dozen micro-settlements, most with populations under 50. Some are stable. Some are dying. Some have turned hostile. Technology has regressed to roughly 19th-century levels with occasional salvaged anachronisms: a solar panel wired to a mud-brick wall, a rifle maintained for three decades with hand-filed replacement parts, a radio built from wreck components by someone who half-remembers how.

Your colonists are the newest arrivals. Their ship — whatever it was, wherever it came from — crashed. They crawled out of the wreck with the clothes on their backs and whatever they could grab. They don't know the planet's history. They don't know about the Silence. They don't know about the ancient infrastructure underneath their feet. They know they're alive, the sun is setting, and something is calling in the dark.

## The Silence

Thirty years ago, the supply ships stopped. Transmissions from the inner systems cut off. The last coherent message was fragmented — the words "quarantine," "closure," and possibly "war" were heard by different operators at different settlements. Nobody agrees on the exact wording.

What happened? The game never answers this definitively. Possibilities the player can piece together from fragments:

- **War.** The competing colonial powers in the inner systems went to war. The outer colonies were cut off as supply lines became military assets. The war ended — or didn't — and nobody bothered to re-establish contact with marginal worlds.
- **Plague.** Something swept through the dense inner systems. Quarantine sealed the corridors between stars. The outer colonies, too far to be affected, were also too far to be helped.
- **Collapse.** The economic system that funded colonial expansion simply... stopped working. No dramatic event. Just a slow unwinding of commitments. Ships stopped being sent because no one was paying for them.
- **Something else.** The ancient builders of this planet's infrastructure — whatever happened to them might have happened to humanity. The hollowcall is still broadcasting. Nobody answered it either.

The ambiguity matters. Different settlements on the planet believe different theories. The military outpost insists it was a war and keeps discipline for the day the fleet returns. The religious settlement believes it was divine judgment. The traders don't care — they deal in what's here, not what's gone. The Redskulls believe civilization deserved to die.

A colonist's theory about the Silence reveals their values. It's a Rorschach test. The player develops their own theory through fragments — radio intercepts, old logs found in ruins, trader gossip — and it colors how they play. Are you rebuilding civilization? Or building something new? Or just surviving until tomorrow?

## Who's Still Here

The planet's surviving human population: roughly 300–500 people in scattered groups. Your colony is one of these. The others exist on the world map (world-and-seasons.md) and interact through trade, radio, and occasionally violence.

### The Settlements

**Dry Creek** — A farming community in the lowlands. Descendants of the agricultural faction that split from Perdition Point. Peaceful, insular, suspicious of strangers. They have food surplus but almost no metal or salvage technology. They barter grain for tools. Their elder remembers the first colony and will tell stories if you earn trust. Population: ~60.

**Fort Morrow** — A military outpost built around a defensible canyon. The remnants of a security detachment from the original charter, still maintaining rank and protocol thirty years later. Disciplined, well-armed (they've maintained their weapons meticulously), but brittle — their numbers are declining and they refuse to adapt their social structure. They trade ammunition and protection for food and labor. The commanding officer still wears the charter insignia. Population: ~40.

**The Crossing** — A trade post at a river ford, run by a family of independent traders. Not a settlement exactly — more a permanent camp that other groups visit. Neutral ground. Information hub. If you want to know what's happening on the planet, The Crossing hears it first. They mark up prices shamelessly because they can. Population: ~15.

**Chapel Hill** — A religious community that has turned deeply inward. They believe the Silence was judgment and that the planet is purgatory. They grow their own food, refuse most trade, and view technology beyond basic tools as sinful. Occasionally a member leaves — or is expelled — and shows up at your colony with nothing but faith and a willingness to work. Population: ~35.

**Perdition Point** (ruins) — The original colony. Now abandoned, partially collapsed, overgrown. The most dangerous and most valuable exploration site on the planet. Deep inside: pre-Silence technology, records, the original charter documents, and access to the ancient infrastructure below. Also: structural collapse, creature nesting, and the persistent legend that someone — or something — still lives in the lower levels.

### The Hostile Factions

**The Redskulls** — Former colonists who abandoned the social contract entirely. They take what they want. Led by someone known only as "Red" — whether that's a name, a title, or a myth depends on who you ask. The Redskulls aren't cartoon villains. They're people who looked at the frontier, at the Silence, at the dying settlements clinging to a covenant written by people who abandoned them, and decided the only honest response was to take what they need by force. Some of them are former military. Some are outcasts from other settlements. Some are second-generation — kids who grew up knowing nothing but raiding. They paint their skulls red because identity is power when you have nothing else. Defeating a Redskull raider and interrogating them might reveal that they grew up in Dry Creek, that they have a sister at Chapel Hill, that they used to farm before a drought year broke them. Or they might spit in your face and lie about everything (social-knowledge.md deception system).

**Scrappers** — Smaller, less organized than Redskulls. Scavengers who strip wrecks and ruins of anything valuable and trade it — or fight over it. Not inherently hostile, but territorial about their salvage sites. A wreck you're exploring might already be "claimed" by a scrapper group. Diplomacy is possible. So is violence. They know the wreck locations better than anyone and can be valuable trading partners if you approach carefully.

**Unknowns** — Occasionally, someone shows up who doesn't fit. Not from any known settlement. Not from a wreck. They speak the same language but with idioms nobody recognizes. They know things about the planet that nobody should know — creature behavior patterns, ancient infrastructure access points, weather prediction beyond any almanac. They don't explain themselves. They might stay a while, trade something valuable, and leave. Or they might be testing you. The game never explains who they are. They are the deepest layer of the mystery — are they descendants of a settlement nobody knew about? Did they emerge from the ancient infrastructure? Are they something else entirely?

## The Ancient Layer

Beneath everything human: the engineered planet (the-human-layer.md). The ancient infrastructure predates humanity by millennia. It was built by something that created this world's ecosystem — the terrain, the atmosphere, the creatures. Whatever built it is gone, but the systems persist. The thermogast is a maintenance organism. The hollowcall is a beacon. The glintcrawlers are pest control.

This layer is not a quest line. It's a slow accumulation of unease. Discoveries happen through digging, through artifact study, through deciphering fragments of an alien language. Each discovery reframes something on the surface: "The duskweavers aren't random predators — they're following patrol routes defined by terrain features that aren't natural." The player never gets a complete picture. The mystery stays open. What happened to the builders? Is it connected to the Silence? The game whispers but never answers.

## How the Setting Enters the Game

The lore above never appears in a codex or a loading screen. It enters through systems the player already interacts with.

### The Radio

Once built, the radio picks up transmissions on different frequencies (frequency slider or preset channels):

- **The Perdition broadcast.** An automated distress signal from the original colony ship, still transmitting after 47 years on a dying battery. Coordinates for a landing site that no longer exists. The voice is calm, professional, from another era. It plays on loop. Always there. The background hum of failure.
- **Settlement chatter.** Dry Creek's weather reports (useful — advance storm warning). Fort Morrow's coded military traffic (decipherable with the cipher key item from DN-018, reveals patrol routes). Chapel Hill's hymns at sunset. The Crossing's trade announcements ("Caravan due in three days, bringing copper and medicine, seeking iron and seeds").
- **Redskull frequencies.** Encrypted, harsh, intermittent. If decoded: raid plans, supply caches, internal disputes. But the encryption changes — it's a constant intelligence game (social-knowledge.md deception system). The Redskulls know you might be listening. Sometimes they broadcast false information on purpose.
- **Ghosts.** Old transmissions from settlements that no longer exist. A woman describing her colony's last winter. A man reading inventory lists to no one. An automated weather station somewhere in the mountains, still faithfully reporting data. These are atmosphere — they make the radio feel like a window into a world that's been dying for decades.
- **The unknown signal.** A transmission on a frequency that shouldn't carry voice. Regular. Patterned. Not human language. Not the hollowcall. Something else. It's been broadcasting longer than humans have been on this planet. Nobody talks about it.

### Traders and Caravans

Traders aren't NPCs with random inventories. They're people from specific settlements with specific needs:

- A trader from Dry Creek arrives with grain, dried meat, and sweetmoss wine. They want iron tools, nails, anything metal. They talk about the weather, the harvest, the duskweavers getting bolder this autumn. They mention that a family left Dry Creek last month — "couldn't take another winter." Where did they go? Nobody knows. Maybe they'll show up at your gate.
- A trader from Fort Morrow arrives with ammunition, a spare rifle (well-maintained, fifty years old), and medical supplies from their dwindling stockpile. They want food — Fort Morrow's canyon has poor soil. They're stiff, formal, call you "civilian." They mention that their commanding officer is getting old. What happens to Fort Morrow when she dies is an open question nobody at the fort will discuss.
- A scrapper shows up unannounced with a cart of salvage: circuit boards, pipe fittings, a half-functional solar panel, an alien fragment they don't understand. They want anything. They'll take food, tools, bullets, clothing. They're cagey about where they found the salvage. They eye your walls. They might come back later with friends — to trade, or not.

Each trader builds (or erodes) a trust relationship over multiple visits. A trader you've dealt with fairly three times brings better goods and shares information freely. One you cheated brings less and tells others. Reputation on a sparsely populated planet is everything — there are only a handful of people who might ever visit you, and they all talk to each other.

### Newcomers at the Gate

People arrive. Not often — every few weeks, sometimes longer. Each one has a story:

- A family from Dry Creek that couldn't make it through another drought. They bring farming knowledge and seed stock but no weapons and no will to fight.
- A deserter from Fort Morrow who couldn't take the discipline anymore. Skilled with weapons, knows military tactics, but will Fort Morrow come looking for them?
- An exile from Chapel Hill who asked too many questions about the Silence. Educated, philosophical, terrible at manual labor. Knows things about the ancient infrastructure from Chapel Hill's suppressed records.
- A lone drifter who claims to be from "another settlement, far north." Nobody has heard of it. They have knowledge that doesn't match any known group. They work hard, don't talk much, and occasionally know things they shouldn't. (An Unknown.)
- A former Redskull who wants out. Scarred, untrusting, dangerous. Are they genuine or a spy? The deception system (DN-019) handles this — but the player has to decide with incomplete information.

Each newcomer is a design decision with consequences. More mouths to feed. But also: more hands, more knowledge domains, more social complexity. And always the question: can you trust them?

### Letters from Before

Colonists carry personal items from before the crash — a letter from a parent, a child's drawing, a photograph (described in text, not shown). These are inventory items with no mechanical value. They appear in the colonist's belongings when you inspect their equipment (DN-018).

Reading a letter is a mood event. The letter describes a life that probably no longer exists: "Your sister had the baby. A girl. Named her after your grandmother." The colonist reads it periodically — each time, a small mood swing. Positive at first (comfort). Then, over months, tinged with grief (loss). Eventually, the colonist might write a reply (at the writing desk) that will never be sent. The reply goes in the library. It's a lore item, but not one with any practical knowledge. It's a human document. Other colonists can read it. It affects their mood — a reminder that everyone here came from somewhere.

If a colonist's letter is destroyed (fire, theft), the mood hit is severe: "Lost my letter from home: -10 mood, 15 days." The letter was their last physical connection to the before.

### The Night Sky

On clear nights, the sky is visible. Stars. The familiar and unfamiliar constellations the colonists have started naming (the-human-layer.md naming system). And: debris.

The wreckage of the Perdition and other ships still orbits. On some nights, a colonist spots a piece catching sunlight — a slow-moving point of light, brighter than a star, that tracks across the sky and vanishes. It's beautiful. It's a reminder that the way up is broken.

Rarely — very rarely — something new moves across the sky. A light that doesn't match any known debris trajectory. A colonist reports: "I saw something. Moving wrong." Is it a ship? A satellite? Another piece of debris on a new orbit? The game never confirms. But it creates hope and dread in equal measure. And it connects to the Unknowns — where did those people come from?

Colonists who work the watchtower at night have a chance of spotting these events. An astronomy knowledge domain could develop over years — colonists who track the sky learn to predict debris passes, which is useful for morale (it's comforting to know the pattern) and occasionally for lore (a debris trajectory doesn't match — it's accelerating, not decelerating. Someone is up there).

### The Wreck You Came From

Your starting wreck is unique to each playthrough. Not just a generic crash site — it was a ship with a purpose, and the wreck contains evidence of what that purpose was:

- **A transport ship.** Civilian passengers heading for an established colony — one that doesn't exist anymore. The wreck contains personal effects, basic tools, and a passenger manifest with more names than survivors. Where did the others go? Some died in the crash. Some might have walked away before you woke up.
- **A cargo hauler.** A shipment of supplies for a settlement that stopped sending pickup requests. The wreck is a treasure trove of materials — but the crew was small, and most of the cargo is damaged. The salvage sustains you for weeks. After that, you need the land.
- **A prison transport.** The Convict backstory isn't just flavor — some of your starting colonists are genuine prisoners. The guards are dead. The restraints are off. The question of who was a prisoner and who was a guard is ambiguous. Nobody volunteers the answer.
- **Something classified.** A military or corporate vessel with sealed containers you can't open (yet). The mechanic recognizes the hull markings but won't say from what organization. The sealed cargo might be weapons, might be research equipment, might be something stranger. Opening it requires metalworking knowledge and patience — and whatever's inside changes the colony's trajectory.

The wreck type could be chosen during map generation or randomized. Each creates a different early game, different lore hooks, and a different relationship between the player and the wider setting.

## The Emotional Register

The setting should feel like a specific set of emotions, not just a list of facts:

**Melancholy, not despair.** The frontier is hard but not hopeless. People build. People laugh in the saloon. Children are born (future mechanic). The sunset is beautiful over the ridge. The melancholy comes from knowing what was lost — the civilization behind you, the people who didn't make it, the letters that will never be answered. But life goes on. Life insists on going on.

**Warmth in small spaces.** Firefly understood this: the ship is home. The crew is family. The universe is cold but the galley is warm. Rayworld should nail this contrast — the harsh alien landscape outside vs. the warm, human, messy, loud, alive colony inside. A winter night with the wind howling and the saloon full of laughter and argument and the smell of Jeb's terrible stew is the emotional core of the game.

**Competence as beauty.** The Mandalorian works because Din Djarin is good at his job. There's a deep satisfaction in watching someone who knows what they're doing. The Expert metallurgist at the smelter, working with casual confidence, not looking at her hands — that's beautiful. The Scout reading terrain, the Doc treating a wound, the Mechanic listening to a broken engine and knowing what's wrong from the sound. Competence IS the aesthetic of the space western.

**Silence as presence.** The old west worked because of the space between things. The long ride. The empty street. The horizon. Rayworld has this built in — the 512×512 map is mostly NOT colony. It's wilderness. It's quiet. The hollowcall carries across empty terrain because there's nothing else to hear. Colonists walking between buildings hear their own footsteps in the sound sim. The silence is a character.

**Mystery without answers.** The ancient infrastructure, the Unknowns, the signal on the impossible frequency, the Silence itself — these are not puzzles to be solved. They're textures that make the world feel deep. The player who digs into the ancient ruins gets more questions, not answers. The feeling is: this world was here before you, and it will be here after you, and you will never fully understand it. Humility before the unknown.

|--------|---------------------------|
| **Trade** (not yet documented) | Not an economy — a web of desperate mutual aid. Settlements trade what they have surplus for what they need to survive. Reputation is everything because there are only a handful of potential partners. |
| **Radio** (the-human-layer.md) | Not just event notifications — a window into a dying world. Transmissions from settlements that might not exist next season. The Perdition broadcast as a 47-year-old ghost. |
| **Newcomers** (chargen.md) | Not random NPCs — refugees from specific settlements with specific histories. The Dry Creek farmer, the Fort Morrow deserter, the Chapel Hill exile. Each one brings context. |
| **Creatures** (alien-fauna.md) | Not just threats — remnant biotech from the ancient builders. Each discovery about their behavior is a breadcrumb toward the deeper mystery. |
| **Ruins** (world-and-seasons.md) | Not just loot sites — layers of history. Human ruins from the first colony. Ancient infrastructure from the builders. Each dig is archaeology. |
| **Knowledge** (DN-019) | Not abstract progression — the desperate preservation of understanding in a world that's losing it. Every lore item is a hedge against the dark. Every Expert is irreplaceable. |
| **Equipment** (DN-018) | Not RPG loot — patched, maintained, inherited gear from a technological civilization that stopped manufacturing. A 50-year-old rifle maintained by three generations. A solar panel wired with hand-twisted copper. |

## What This Doesn't Need

- **No codex.** The lore enters through gameplay, not menus.
- **No quest log.** The player's goals emerge from situation, not from objectives.
- **No definitive answers.** The Silence, the builders, the Unknowns — mystery stays mysterious.
- **No villain monologue.** The Redskulls have no boss who explains their ideology. You piece it together from prisoner interrogations and overheard radio transmissions.
- **No chosen one.** Your colony is not special. You're one of several groups of survivors on a forgotten planet. The only thing that makes you matter is that you're still alive.
- **No galactic stakes.** The universe is far away and possibly dead. The stakes are: do these 8 people survive the winter? That's enough. That's everything.

## Summary

The setting is a sparsely populated planet 47 years into a failed colonization, 30 years past losing contact with the wider universe. The original colony fractured and declined. A handful of settlements survive, each with different cultures, needs, and relationships to the past. Hostile factions exist because desperation creates them. An ancient alien infrastructure lies beneath everything, unexplained and unexplainable.

Your colonists are the newest arrivals — crashed, confused, alone. They learn the planet's human history through trade, radio, and exploration. They learn its deeper history through digging. They learn each other through shared survival.

The emotional register is Firefly's warmth in a cold universe, the Mandalorian's competence as beauty, the old west's silence and distance, and RimWorld's "these are your people and they might die tonight." The setting doesn't need exposition. It needs presence — the Perdition broadcast looping in the background, the debris glinting in the night sky, the letter from home that will never be answered, the hollowcall echoing across terrain that was shaped by hands that weren't human.

The planet was here before you. It will be here after you. Your job is to make the in-between matter.
